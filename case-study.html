Waste-Less

Case Study • No. 1

Project Overview

Waste-Less is a web app (Hackathon MVP) that helps households track groceries, split bulk purchases, and generate recipes that use what's already in the pantry - reducing food waste and saving money. The app highlights items that were previously wasted and prioritizes them in the next shopping list; it also supports bulk-splitting for cheaper groceries

User Research & Discovery

Exploring Ideas & Choosing the Problem

Our hackathon theme challenged us to design solutions with social impact. We brainstormed a wide range of ideas, but the one that resonated most strongly was dividing groceries between multiple users to reduce household food waste.

Through discussion, we realized that food waste wasn't just a household problem - it connected to bigger issues: rising grocery cost, market price manipulation, and the sustainability of local producers. This led us to develop the first draft of our app's purpose:

  • Track the average calories consumed by a household and alert if purchases exceed typical consumption
  • Suggest recipes that use leftover groceries
  • Prioritze previously wasted items in the next shopping list
  • Future feature: optimize recipes based on what users liked most

From here, we defined core functionalities:

  • Main Page with clear calls to action to sign up or log in
  • Groceries Page to add, edit, delete and mark items as used/wasted, with a bulk-splitting feature
  • Recipe List with search, AI-generated suggestions and saved recipes
  • Waste Tracking that builds smarter shopping lsits

Research Insights

1. Food Waste & Loss

We grounded our problem statement in global and national research on food waste.

  • The UN Food Waste Index Report 2024 highlights that 1.05 billion tonnes of food were wasted globally in 2022, with 60% occuring at the household level.
  • The FAO SDG Indicators Portal emphasizes the urgency of halving global food waste by 2030 (SDG 12.3).
  • Household waste is key driver - showing that an app focusing on family/roommate grocery management could create meaningful impact.

Key insights: Most waste comes from households, not farms or retail - making consumer-focused solutions the most impactful intervention.

2. Consumer Pricing & Costs

We also examined the economic pressures that influence grocery shopping.

  • The egg price-fixing lawsuit revealed systemic manipulation in the food industry, with $17.7 in damage awarded against Cal-Maine Foods, Kraft and others.
  • The Canadian Competition Bureau's 2024 study identified limited competition in the grocery sector, driving higher prices for consumers.
  • Consumers are pushed toward bulk purchases (e.g., Costco, big-box chains) as a cost-saving strategy, even if it increases the risk of household waste.

Key insights: Shoppers often over-purchase to save money upfront, but this leads to waste that negates the savings.

3. Local Farmers & Producers

Sustainability also shaped our thinking.

  • Loblaw's controversial “No Frills Farmers' market” campaign sparked backlash about corporate grocery chains undercutting real local farmers.
  • The Competition Bureau's grocery innovation strategy called for new types of grocery business, unit pricing transparency and stronger support for the independent grocers.
  • Statista data shows that Canada's grocery market share is highly concentrated, with just a few retailers dominating.

Key insights: Supporting alternative grocery models (like bulk-sharing or independent grocers) has both economic and sustainability benefits.

Summary of Research Takeaways

From global waste data to Canadian grocery economics, our research showed:

  • Households are the largest contributors to food waste.
  • Price manipulation and limited grocery competition push consumers toward bulk-buying.
  • Bulk purchases save money upfront but often lead to spoilage in the home.
  • Consumers want fairer, more transparent systems that benefit both them and producers.

Problem Statement

Food waste is a global crisis - in 2022 alone, over 1 billion tonnes of food were wasted, with households responsible for 60% of it. At the same time, Canadians face high grocery costs from concentrated markets and price manipulation. The result: families and individuals over-purchase to save money, yet waste both food and income in the process.

Brainstorming

During ideation, our team explored several directions related to the hackathon theme of sustainability and food systems. We generated ideas around automation, shared purchasing, and efficiency in meal preparation, then evaluated which had the most impact and feasibility for a hackathon MVP.


1. Automatic Fridge & Pantry Tracker (Recipe Book)

  • Concept: A system that automatically logs what's in a fridge/pantry and suggests recipes based on available items
  • Benefit: Reduces “forgotten items” that spoil in the back of the fridge
  • Challenge: Requires hardware integration or barcode scanning, which was too complex for a hackathon timeline

2. Wishlist/Online Costco (Bulk Splitting)

  • Concept: Households or roommates can divide bulk products (e.g., 40-roll toilet paper packs) and split costs digitally
  • Benefit: Makes bulk buying more affordable and less wasteful
  • Challenge: Needed an intuitive way to coordinate between users in real time

3. Bulk Cooking Planner

  • Concept: Helps households prepare large batches of meals at once and portion them out across the week
  • Benefit: Saves time and ensures groceries are used efficiently
  • Challenge: Relies on user commitment to cooking routines

4. Smart Grocery Deals + Purchase Tracker

  • Concept: Cross-references grocery store deals with pantry stock to suggest the best items to purchase
  • Benefit: Saves money while preventing over-purchasing
  • Challenge: Integration with multiple grocery APIs would be complex to implement

We combined the most feasible and impactful aspects of these ideas into our final concept: Waste-Less. Instead of building hardware or connecting to grocery APIs, we focused on tracking groceries, splitting bulk purchases, and generating recipes - features we could realistically prototype in Figma and implement in a working MVP within the hackathon timeline.

Reflection & Lessons Learned

Working on Waste-Less during a hackathon pushed us to balance ambition with feasibility. Our initial ideas included hardware integrations and real-time grocery store APIs, but we quickly realized the need to focus on features we could design, test, and implement in 48 hours.

Key lessons we learned:

  • Prioritize core flows first. By focusing on the simplest path (add groceries → mark used/wasted → generate recipe), we created a functional MVP instead of spreading ourselves too thin.
  • Design for clarity. Accessibility and clean navigation mattered more than flashy visuals, since our users needed to quickly scan items and take action.
  • Collaboration is key. As a team of strangers meeting at a hackathon, we learned how to quickly align on tasks and leverage each other's strengths (design, coding, research, pitching).
  • Impact needs simplicity. The most compelling parts of our pitch weren't the “future features” but the clear, actionable MVP that solved a real problem households face daily.

The Design Brief

Project Goals & Target Audience

Waste-Less was designed to help budget-conscious households, families, and roommates reduce food waste and grocery costs. Our target users include:

  • Individuals living alone who struggle with portion sizes.
  • Families buying in bulk to save money but struggling with spoilage.
  • Roommates who need a fair and transparent way to share grocery expenses.

Rationale Behind UI Decisions

  • Layout: We chose a fixed header, sidebar navigation, and consistent footer to provide structure and make core features (groceries, recipes, waste tracking) easily accessible.
  • Typography: Clean, legible fonts ensured quick readability during task-heavy interactions, such as marking groceries used or wasted.
  • Colour Palette: We used a modern, clean base with accents of green (sustainability, “used” items, positive actions) and red (waste, alerts, negative states). This visual language made the app intuitive and aligned with the problem we were solving.
  • Accessibility: We emphasized contrast ratios, responsive layouts, and clear button placement so users could interact smoothly across devices.
